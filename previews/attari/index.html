The Founder's Letter

A patron's love letter to the village her family founded.

My name is Primal Sidhu. I was born in Attari, a village my own ancestors built on a green plain three kilometres from the line that now divides India and Pakistan. I left as a teenager. I never stopped belonging to it.

Around the year 1740, two Sidhu brothers, Gaur Singh and Kaur Singh, walked north from Ludhiana, took the Khande De Pahul at the Akal Takhat in Amritsar, and at the suggestion of an ascetic named Mool Dass, raised a village on a quiet mound in the Majha. They called it Attari. The village has carried the Sidhu name for nearly three centuries. I carry it still.

For decades I have lived in Seattle, half a world from the dust on the road to my grandmother's house. But the road never left me. Every season, every harvest, every face I cannot forget walks back to me from a place I now visit only in memory and in love.

This site is a small thing for so vast a feeling. It is a record of a village. It is a promise that as long as I am able, the place my forebears built will not be forgotten by anyone who comes here looking for it. I am not a foundation. I am not a charity. I am one daughter of the Sidhu line, choosing to be the patron her village deserves, alone if she has to be.

Primal Sidhu, Founder & Patron

A Place On The Map

Attari, Punjab

Founded by the Sidhus

A village raised by two brothers, around 1740.

Attari was founded in roughly 1740 by Gaur Singh and Kaur Singh, two Sidhu Jat brothers who had crossed north from Kaonke in Ludhiana. They took the Khande De Pahul at the Akal Takhat in Amritsar, and on the counsel of a local ascetic named Mool Dass, they raised the village on a quiet earthen mound, a "theh," in the heart of the Majha. The Sidhu name has been folded into Attari ever since.

A General's Hometown

Attari gave Punjab one of its great soldiers.

The village is the native home of Sardar Sham Singh Attariwala, one of the celebrated generals of Maharaja Ranjit Singh's Sikh Empire. The British, surveying the Majha in the 1860s, called the Sikh Sardars of Attari "the best blood of the Manjha." The reputation was earned in battle, in court, and in the long memory of Punjab.

Land & Harvest

Wheat and rice on the alluvial plain.

Attari farms the rice-wheat rotation typical of Punjab's fertile soils. Mustard turns the fields gold in February, wheat ripens in April, rice follows the rains. Punjabi is the spoken tongue here, written in Gurmukhi, and the Majhi dialect particular to this stretch of the Amritsar plain still flavours the way the village greets the morning.

A Border, Drawn Overnight

In 1947, the world moved. Attari did not.

When the Radcliffe Line was published on 17 August 1947, Attari woke up an Indian border village. The neighbouring village of Wagah, once part of the same continuous Punjabi countryside, was placed in Pakistan. Days later, two old army friends, Brigadier Mohinder Singh Chopra of India and Brigadier Nazir Ahmed of Pakistan, met at the line, set down stones in drums, and marked a border with a small swing gate. Attari has lived alongside that gate ever since.

Heritage & Culture

The threads that still hold.

Six small windows into the world that shaped a girl who would one day, from very far away, decide to take care of it.

01

The Beating Retreat

Every evening since 1959, the BSF and the Pakistan Rangers have lowered the flags together at the Wagah-Attari gate. The drill is fierce. The handshake is daily. The dust on both sides is the same dust.

02

The Gurdwara & Langar

A place of prayer, yes, but also a kitchen, a school, a refuge. The langar feeds anyone who walks through the door, no caste, no faith, no questions. In Attari, this is the rule, not the exception.

03

Sham Singh Attariwala

The general who fell at Sabraon in 1846, fighting for the Sikh Empire. He was a son of this village. Centuries on, his name is still spoken in Attari with the quiet pride reserved for one's own.

04

The Mustard Fields

In February the plain turns yellow from horizon to horizon. By April it is green wheat. By August it is rice and rain. The Punjab year is read in colour long before any calendar is consulted.

05

Vaisakhi

Spring harvest, Sikh new year, all in one day. Nagar kirtan winds through the lanes, gatka swords flash, and the langar pots will not be empty until the last guest is fed. April 13 or 14, every single year.

06

Bandi Chhor Divas

The Sikh observance that falls on Diwali, marking Guru Hargobind's release from Mughal captivity in 1619. The gurdwara is lit. Pinni and karah prasad are passed around. The fields glow with lamps.

Three Centuries Of Attari

From 1740, to a partition, and home again.

The Sidhu line, the village, the border, and the daughter who came back to look after all three.

c. 1740
Founding of Attari

Two brothers raise a village.

Gaur Singh and Kaur Singh Sidhu, sons of Chaudhri Kahn Chand of Kaonke, walk north into the Majha. They take the Khande De Pahul at the Akal Takhat in Amritsar and, on the counsel of an ascetic named Mool Dass, raise a village on a quiet earthen mound. They call it Attari. The Sidhu line is rooted in this soil.

1846
The General Falls

Sham Singh Attariwala dies at Sabraon.

The Anglo-Sikh Wars come to the village by way of its most famous son. Sardar Sham Singh Attariwala, general of Maharaja Ranjit Singh's army, is killed at the Battle of Sabraon. His name is etched into Attari permanently, the way a wound becomes part of the body.

1947
A Border, Overnight

The Radcliffe Line passes between Attari and Wagah.

On 17 August, the new boundary is published. Attari, an interior Punjabi village a moment before, is now a border village. Days later, two old army friends, one Indian, one Pakistani, place stones in drums on the Grand Trunk Road and mark the line with a small swing gate. It is still there.

1959
The Beating Retreat Begins

The flag-lowering ceremony, codified.

The BSF and the Pakistan Rangers begin a daily ritual at the gate that will, in time, become one of the most-watched military ceremonies in the world. On Eid and Diwali, the soldiers exchange sweets across the line. They have done so, with interruptions, for more than sixty years.

A Childhood
Attari, In Living Memory

A Sidhu girl grows up at the edge of the country.

Primal Sidhu is born and raised in Attari. The village she walks home through every evening is the village her ancestors raised. She leaves as a teenager, the way a great many Punjabi children do, with the village folded carefully into her language and her cooking.

Today
Seattle, To Attari

A patron's decision, made alone.

From a city on the other side of the world, the daughter of Attari quietly returns. Not as a foundation. Not as a charity. As one woman of the Sidhu line, choosing to be the patron her village deserves. This site is the public face of that promise.

The Mission

A patron's quiet work.

No board. No fundraising. No campaigns. One person, three commitments, a village.

i.

To Remember

To gather, in one place, the photographs, voices, and small inheritances of a village that has never had a website written in its honour.

ii.

To Sustain

To direct private support, in modest, deliberate ways, toward the people and projects of Attari that need it most. Quietly, by hand, with no public fanfare.

iii.

To Pass On

To leave behind, for any child of Attari with a question, a record clear enough that they will not have to start their own search from nothing.

Get In Touch

If Attari belongs to you too.

If you grew up here. If your family came from here. If you have a photograph, a name, a memory, a recording, anything at all that belongs to the story of Attari, this site is open to you. Write privately. Send what you have. It will be received with care.
